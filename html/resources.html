Current Reality

The forceful expulsion of exiled Bhutanese, also known as Lhotshampas, from their homes in the early 1990s is considered a major human rights violation. The Bhutanese government, which is dominated by the Drukpa ethnic group, targeted the Lhotshampas, who are primarily of Nepali origin, claiming that they posed a threat to Bhutanese culture and national identity. As a result, the government began a campaign of intimidation and violence, forcing tens of thousands of Lhotshampas to flee to neighboring Nepal. Many of these exiles were subjected to harsh conditions in refugee camps and faced discrimination and restrictions on their rights, including restrictions on their ability to work, access healthcare and education, and participate in political activities.

Additionally, many exiled Bhutanese who were captured and imprisoned by the Bhutanese government faced torture and other forms of mistreatment, resulting in numerous deaths. Furthermore, many Lhotshampas are still missing and their whereabouts are unknown, leaving their families and loved ones in a state of uncertainty and distress. The ongoing human rights violations against the exiled Bhutanese have led to calls for justice and accountability, as well as efforts to secure their rights to return to their homes and land in Bhutan. The situation of the exiled Bhutanese serves as a reminder of the importance of protecting the rights of all individuals, regardless of their ethnicity or nationality, and the need for governments to uphold their obligations under international human rights law.

Thousands of Bhutanese families have been separated since their forceful expulsion. More than 110,000 Bhutanese refugees have been resettled in western countries, but they are not able to reunify or meet with their loved ones. Their parents are dying without meeting their children and vice versa or are unable to be present in their last rituals. Unfortunately, the government of Bhutan does not allow resettled Bhutanese to visit their native country, further adding to the pain of separation.

It is important to recognize that family unification plays a critical role in the happiness and well-being of the Bhutanese people. The separation of families caused by the Bhutanese refugee crisis has resulted in immense emotional distress and suffering, leaving many individuals and communities without the support and care of their loved ones. Therefore, prioritizing family reunification and finding ways to facilitate this process should be a key focus of any efforts to address the crisis. By working towards reuniting families and providing support for their integration and well-being, it may be possible to improve the mental health and overall happiness of the Bhutanese people. Ultimately, a society that values and prioritizes family unity and well-being can lead to a more prosperous and harmonious community for all.
